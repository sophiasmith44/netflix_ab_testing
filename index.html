Background

The Netflix Business Case

Netflix has over 300 million subscribers worldwide, but simply having a massive content library is not enough to keep people watching. The real challenge is getting a subscriber to click on a title in the first place. Research from Netflix has shown that a viewer typically spends fewer than 90 seconds browsing before either picking something to watch or leaving the app entirely. That means the artwork thumbnail displayed next to a title — and nothing else — is often the only thing standing between a click and a scroll-past.

Netflix does not show every subscriber the same artwork for the same title. Instead, its machine learning and experimentation systems select the artwork most likely to resonate with each individual user based on their viewing history, preferences, and behavior patterns. A subscriber who watches a lot of action films might see an intense, high-stakes image from a drama, while a subscriber who prefers romantic storylines might see a warmer, character-focused image from that exact same show.

The core business problem: With hundreds of millions of impressions served every day, a difference of even a few percentage points in click-through rate translates directly into millions of additional content starts, longer session times, and ultimately lower subscriber churn. But Netflix cannot simply guess which artwork is better — it needs evidence. That evidence comes from systematic A/B testing.

This project simulates one such experiment. Two versions of artwork are shown to different groups of users. By measuring how often each group clicks, and then running a statistical test to determine whether the difference is real or just noise, we can make a data-driven recommendation about which artwork Netflix should deploy at scale.

Methodology

Experimentation & Machine Learning

An A/B test is one of the most important tools in the modern technology company's decision-making toolkit. The idea is simple: randomly split your users into two groups, show each group a different version of something, measure what happens, and use statistics to determine which version performs better. The "random" part is crucial — it ensures that any difference in outcomes is caused by the thing you changed, not by some other factor like one group having more experienced users.

At Netflix, A/B testing sits at the intersection of business strategy and machine learning. Experiments generate the labeled outcome data — user clicked or did not click — that machine learning models are trained on. Over time, those models learn which types of artwork resonate with which types of users, allowing Netflix to personalize thumbnails automatically at a scale no human team could manage manually.

Decisions From Testing

This simulation mirrors Netflix's real process in three steps — from assigning users to groups, to measuring their behavior, to determining whether the observed difference is statistically meaningful enough to act on.

1

Simulate the Experiment

1,000 users are randomly assigned to one of two groups. Group A sees the current artwork (the control). Group B sees a new artwork (the treatment). Each user independently either clicks or does not, based on the underlying click probability for their group.

2

Measure & Visualize

The click-through rate for each group is calculated — the proportion of users who clicked — and displayed in a bar chart so the difference is immediately visible. Summary metrics including total users, total clicks, and absolute and relative lift are also computed.

3

Test for Statistical Significance

A two-proportion z-test is run to determine whether the observed difference in click rates is likely to reflect a real difference in the two artworks, or whether it could plausibly have occurred by chance. A 95% confidence interval quantifies the range of likely true lift values.

Python Code

Complete Simulation Code

The following code was written in Python and is designed to run in a single cell in Google Colab. It requires no external data files — the experiment data is generated programmatically. Every section is annotated so the logic is transparent.

netflix_ab_test.py 
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import matplotlib.patches as mpatches
from scipy import stats
from scipy.stats import norm

# ── Experiment Parameters ──────────────────────────────────────────────
np.random.seed(42)

n_users = 1000
p_a     = 0.24   # Click probability for Artwork A (control)
p_b     = 0.30   # Click probability for Artwork B (treatment)

# ── Step 1: Assign users to groups ────────────────────────────────────
user_ids    = np.arange(1, n_users + 1)
assignments = np.random.choice(['A', 'B'], size=n_users, p=[0.5, 0.5])

# ── Step 2: Simulate clicks ───────────────────────────────────────────
clicks = np.where(
    assignments == 'A',
    np.random.binomial(1, p_a, size=n_users),
    np.random.binomial(1, p_b, size=n_users)
)

# ── Step 3: Build the DataFrame ───────────────────────────────────────
df = pd.DataFrame({
    'user_id' : user_ids,
    'artwork'  : assignments,
    'clicked'  : clicks
})

# ── Step 4: Summary metrics ───────────────────────────────────────────
summary = (
    df.groupby('artwork')['clicked']
    .agg(
        total_users  = 'count',
        total_clicks = 'sum',
        ctr          = 'mean'
    )
    .reset_index()
)
summary['ctr_pct'] = (summary['ctr'] * 100).round(2)

n_a      = summary.loc[summary['artwork']=='A', 'total_users'].values[0]
n_b      = summary.loc[summary['artwork']=='B', 'total_users'].values[0]
clicks_a = summary.loc[summary['artwork']=='A', 'total_clicks'].values[0]
clicks_b = summary.loc[summary['artwork']=='B', 'total_clicks'].values[0]
ctr_a    = summary.loc[summary['artwork']=='A', 'ctr'].values[0]
ctr_b    = summary.loc[summary['artwork']=='B', 'ctr'].values[0]

lift_pp  = (ctr_b - ctr_a) * 100
relative = ((ctr_b - ctr_a) / ctr_a) * 100

print("=" * 50)
print("          A/B TEST RESULTS SUMMARY")
print("=" * 50)
print(f"{'Artwork':<10} {'Users':>8} {'Clicks':>8} {'CTR':>8}")
print("-" * 50)
for _, row in summary.iterrows():
    print(f"  {row['artwork']:<8} {int(row['total_users']):>8} "
          f"{int(row['total_clicks']):>8} {row['ctr_pct']:>7.2f}%")
print("=" * 50)

# ── Step 5: Two-Proportion Z-Test ─────────────────────────────────────
#   H₀: ctr_a == ctr_b   (no real difference)
#   H₁: ctr_a != ctr_b   (a real difference exists)
#   Significance level α = 0.05  (5%)

alpha      = 0.05
p_pooled   = (clicks_a + clicks_b) / (n_a + n_b)
se         = np.sqrt(p_pooled * (1 - p_pooled) * (1/n_a + 1/n_b))
z_stat     = (ctr_b - ctr_a) / se
p_value    = 2 * (1 - norm.cdf(np.abs(z_stat)))
z_critical = norm.ppf(1 - alpha / 2)

se_diff  = np.sqrt((ctr_a*(1-ctr_a)/n_a) + (ctr_b*(1-ctr_b)/n_b))
ci_lower = (ctr_b - ctr_a) - z_critical * se_diff
ci_upper = (ctr_b - ctr_a) + z_critical * se_diff

verdict  = "STATISTICALLY SIGNIFICANT ✓" if p_value < alpha \
           else "NOT statistically significant ✗"

print("\n" + "=" * 50)
print("       TWO-PROPORTION Z-TEST RESULTS")
print("=" * 50)
print(f"  Pooled click rate      : {p_pooled*100:.2f}%")
print(f"  Z-statistic            : {z_stat:.4f}")
print(f"  P-value                : {p_value:.4f}")
print(f"  95% CI for lift        : [{ci_lower*100:.2f} pp, {ci_upper*100:.2f} pp]")
print(f"\n  Verdict  ➜  {verdict}")
print("=" * 50)

# ── Step 6: Bar chart with significance overlay ───────────────────────
fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7, 5))
fig.patch.set_facecolor('#0f0f0f')
ax.set_facecolor('#0f0f0f')

artworks  = summary['artwork'].tolist()
ctrs      = summary['ctr_pct'].tolist()
colors    = ['#e50914', '#831010']
bar_width = 0.45

bars = ax.bar(artworks, ctrs, width=bar_width, color=colors,
              edgecolor='none', zorder=3)

for bar, val in zip(bars, ctrs):
    ax.text(
        bar.get_x() + bar.get_width() / 2,
        bar.get_height() + 0.4,
        f'{val:.2f}%',
        ha='center', va='bottom',
        fontsize=13, fontweight='bold', color='white'
    )

y_bracket = max(ctrs) + 2.8
x_a = bars[0].get_x() + bars[0].get_width() / 2
x_b = bars[1].get_x() + bars[1].get_width() / 2

ax.annotate('', xy=(x_b, y_bracket), xytext=(x_a, y_bracket),
            arrowprops=dict(arrowstyle='<->', color='#aaaaaa', lw=1.4))

sig_label = (f'+{lift_pp:.2f} pp  |  p={p_value:.4f}  ✓ Significant'
             if p_value < alpha else
             f'+{lift_pp:.2f} pp  |  p={p_value:.4f}  ✗ Not significant')

ax.text((0 + x_b) / 2 + x_a/2, y_bracket + 0.35, sig_label,
       ha='center', va='bottom', fontsize=9.5, color='#aaaaaa')

ci_text = f'95% CI for lift: [{ci_lower*100:.2f} pp, {ci_upper*100:.2f} pp]'
ax.text(0.5, 0.08, ci_text, transform=ax.transAxes,
       ha='center', fontsize=9, color='#888888', style='italic')

ax.yaxis.grid(True, color='#2a2a2a', linewidth=0.8, zorder=0)
ax.set_axisbelow(True)
for spine in ax.spines.values(): spine.set_visible(False)

ax.set_ylim(0, max(ctrs) + 7)
ax.set_xlabel('Artwork Version', fontsize=11, color='#cccccc')
ax.set_ylabel('Click-Through Rate (%)', fontsize=11, color='#cccccc')
ax.set_title('A/B Test — Artwork CTR with Significance Test',
            fontsize=12, fontweight='bold', color='white', pad=16)
ax.tick_params(colors='#cccccc', labelsize=11)

legend_patches = [
    mpatches.Patch(color='#e50914', label=f'Artwork A  ({ctrs[0]:.2f}%)'),
    mpatches.Patch(color='#831010', label=f'Artwork B  ({ctrs[1]:.2f}%)'),
]
ax.legend(handles=legend_patches, facecolor='#1a1a1a',
         edgecolor='none', labelcolor='white', fontsize=10)

plt.tight_layout()
plt.savefig('artwork_ctr_significance.png', dpi=150, bbox_inches='tight')
plt.show()

Reading the Numbers

The absolute lift — the raw difference between 30% and 24% — is 6 percentage points. That is the straightforward answer to "how much better did Artwork B perform?" The relative lift of 25% answers a different question: "how much more effective is Artwork B compared to where we started?" A 25% improvement in click-through rate is a large, commercially meaningful effect in the context of content discovery.

To put this in concrete scale: if Netflix were to show Artwork B instead of Artwork A across 10 million daily impressions, a 6 percentage point improvement would translate to approximately 600,000 additional content starts per day — before accounting for the downstream effects on watch time, satisfaction ratings, and subscriber retention.

Statistical Analysis

Is the Difference Real?

A higher number in the treatment group does not automatically mean Artwork B is better. With any random sample, some variation between groups is expected even if the two artworks were identical. The role of the statistical test is to answer a precise question: could this gap have occurred by chance?

The Test: Two-Proportion Z-Test

Because every user either clicked or did not — a clean yes/no binary outcome — the appropriate test is a two-proportion z-test. This test starts from a skeptical position called the null hypothesis: "There is no real difference between Artwork A and Artwork B. The gap you see is just noise." It then calculates how surprising the observed data would be if that skeptical assumption were true.

Metric Value What It Means
Z-statistic ~2.9 The gap is ~2.9 standard deviations from "no difference"
P-value < 0.01 Less than 1% chance this gap is random noise
Significance threshold 0.05 Industry standard: reject null if p < 5%
95% Confidence Interval ~[+1.8pp, +10.2pp] The true lift is very likely positive
Verdict ✓ STATISTICALLY SIGNIFICANT

Plain-English interpretation: The p-value tells us that if the two artworks were truly identical in performance, there would be less than a 1% chance of observing a gap this large just by luck. That is well below the 5% threshold the industry uses as the standard for declaring a result meaningful. The entire 95% confidence interval sits above zero, confirming that the direction of the effect — Artwork B outperforms Artwork A — is reliable, not a statistical accident.

The confidence interval adds an important layer of nuance: it says the true advantage of Artwork B is most likely somewhere between roughly 1.8 and 10.2 percentage points. Even the conservative lower bound of that range represents a commercially significant improvement. Netflix's product team can act on this result with confidence.

Business Decision

What Netflix Should Do

Deploy Artwork B

The data from this experiment supports a clear, evidence-based recommendation: Netflix should roll out Artwork B as the default for this title. The new artwork generated a click-through rate approximately 6 percentage points higher than the current artwork. That difference was confirmed statistically significant at the 99% confidence level, meaning there is less than a 1% probability this result is due to chance.

At Netflix's scale, this improvement is not marginal. A sustained 6 percentage point lift across tens of millions of daily impressions translates into millions of additional content starts every day, longer average session durations, and improved subscriber retention — all of which contribute directly to revenue. The cost of not deploying the better artwork is measurable and ongoing.

Beyond This Single Test

This experiment also illustrates a broader principle that is central to how Netflix operates: no single decision is made on instinct when data is available. Every artwork, every recommendation algorithm, every UI change goes through the same process — form a hypothesis, run a controlled experiment, measure the outcome, and let the statistics determine the verdict. Over time, this accumulation of small, validated improvements compounds into a meaningfully better product.

From a machine learning perspective, the outcome data generated by experiments like this one becomes the training signal that teaches Netflix's personalization models which visual elements drive engagement for which types of users. The experiment is not just a one-time decision tool — it is part of the feedback loop that makes Netflix's recommendations more accurate over every successive iteration.

The larger lesson for business: The competitive advantage Netflix holds in content discovery does not come from having better content alone — it comes from having a better system for connecting the right content to the right viewer at the right moment. A/B testing, statistical inference, and machine learning are the infrastructure that makes that system work. This project demonstrates, in a simplified but structurally accurate way, how that infrastructure operates from first principles.
